# Stage 2 — Cofounders & the Founding Team

*Sources: L2 (Altman), L11 (Collisons/Silbermann), L9 (Conway/Andreessen), L18 (Nathoo/Levy).*

Cofounder relationships are among the most important in the company. **Cofounder blowups are the #1 cause of early startup death.** Treat choosing a cofounder with *more* care than any hire.

## Choosing cofounders

- **Know them for a while — ideally years.** College or working at a good company are the best sources. A random "cofounder dating" match is very high-risk (one YC batch: 9/9 teams that added a stranger cofounder fell apart within a year).
- **Better no cofounder than a bad one** — but solo is still hard (top YC companies almost all have 2+ founders; ~1/10 solo funding rate). **2–3 is ideal**; 1 not great, 4 sometimes, 5 bad.
- **Want James-Bond types**: unflappable, tough, calm, decisive, resourceful ("relentlessly resourceful") — more than a narrow domain expert.
- **Technical company → technical cofounder.** Don't plan to "just hire engineers and be great managers."
- Find someone **as good as or better than you** (Conway: Airbnb's three equally-strong founders were a rare superpower). Look for people who **reason toward truth together** (Max walking into Peter's office with "here's what will kill us"), not who just agree.

## Equity split (do this EARLY)

- Set it **soon after you start working together** — within the first weeks. It does not get easier with time.
- **Near-equal.** Execution >> the idea (ideas have ~zero standalone value). Don't over-reward the "idea" founder or pre-formation work. If everyone's in 100% for the long haul, what happened before formation shouldn't matter. A very disproportionate split is a **red flag** to investors (signals distrust or someone not all-in). Zero top-YC companies have a significantly disproportionate founder split.
- **Vesting for everyone, including you.** Standard: **4 years with a 1-year cliff** (25% at one year, then monthly). This pre-negotiates what happens if a founder leaves — without it, a departing cofounder walks off with dead equity that makes you unfundable. YC won't fund founders without vesting. Even solo founders should vest (skin in the game + sets the example for employees).
- Actually **buy your stock** (Stock Purchase Agreement) and **file your 83(b) election within 30 days — keep proof** (unfixable; has blown up deals). See `09-legal-and-scaling.md`.

## Hiring the first ~10 (once you truly must — see below)

Don't hire until you desperately need to. But when you do, these are effectively near-cofounders.

- **The hardest hires you'll make** (nobody's heard of you; friends tell candidates not to join). Think like a **value investor**: find human capital the market *undervalues* — early-career or not-yet-obvious talent — not already-"discovered" people. Draw from everywhere (referrals, tech talks, communities, even the coffee shop next door). Each of the first 10 effectively recruits the next ~10 via their network.
- **3 questions:** Are they smart? Do they get things done? Do I want to be around them? Best signal = **work together on a real project for 1–2 days** (beats interviews/brainteasers). Ask about past projects; **call references and press** ("top 5% of people you've worked with? would you hire again? why aren't you?").
- Traits that predicted success (Collisons/Silbermann): genuine/straight (trusted, want to work with), **cares a great deal** (offended when something's slightly off), **finishes things**, high integrity, low ego, curious. Communication skill correlates with working out. Want risk-tolerance + maniacal determination (Graham's "animal test").
- Be **radically transparent about how hard it'll be** — hard problems attract the best and let the wrong people self-select out. Don't sell certainty.
- **Experience** matters for senior roles, less for early hires — hire for aptitude and belief. Interview in a way that works for *you*; beat imposter syndrome.

## Equity for employees

- Give ~**10% to the first 10 employees** (4-yr vesting). Keep giving generously *every year* (refresher grants). Founders are wrongly stingy with employees and generous with investors — reverse it. Investors give bad advice here; the most successful companies are generous with employee equity. (More in `08-hiring-culture-management.md`.)

## Try NOT to hire (the counterintuitive default)

- Few employees = pride, not shame (low burn, speed, simplicity, fast decisions). Be proud of how much you do with few people.
- A **single mediocre hire in the first ~5 can kill the startup.** Bar: "Would I bet the company on this hire?" When you *are* in hiring mode, it's your #1 priority (~25% of your time).

## Firing (fire fast)

- Every first-timer waits too long. Fire fast when someone **consistently gets every decision wrong** (obvious over weeks — not one-off mistakes). Also fire people who create **politics** or are **persistently negative** — toxic to a startup.
- Do it right (L18): fire quickly and face-to-face with a third party present; be honest (usually it was a hiring/matching mistake by you — not "you suck," not mushy); take their job, not their dignity (your people are their references); pay all wages + accrued vacation immediately; cut off digital access; repurchase vested shares.

## Location & remote (L2, L13)

- Co-located founders strongly outperform; be skeptical of remote *cofounders* in the early days — communication/speed outweigh everything and video calls underperform.
- Great founders go where the **network for their task** is strongest (Hoffman): SV for software/mobile/marketplaces; elsewhere for other kinds (Groupon needed Chicago's sales-force culture).

## Exit gate → Stage 3

Advance when: 2–3 aligned cofounders you trust, equity split near-equal and signed with vesting, and you're set up to talk to users. Cofounder alignment and communication are never "done" — talk about tension early, don't let it fester.
