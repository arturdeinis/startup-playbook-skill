# Stage 3 — Talk to Users & Become the Expert

*Sources: L16 (Shear), L4 (Cheung), L1 (Altman), L3 (Graham).*

You don't need expertise in *startups*; you need expertise in your **users** (Graham). Zuckerberg succeeded despite being a startup noob because he understood his users. This stage overlaps Stage 4 (build) permanently: at any moment you should be either building the product or talking to users.

## First, immerse in the industry (Cheung)

- State the problem in **one sentence.** Check: Is it a problem *I* have? Am I passionate? Do others have it? (verify by talking to people — don't build for users you'd never be, like their life-coach mistake).
- **Become a cog in the industry for 1–2 months.** The Homejoy founders got jobs cleaning houses / at a cleaning company — the *details* reveal the inefficiencies to exploit. If there's a service element, do the service yourself.
- Be **obsessive**: list every competitor big and small, read every article, S-1s, earnings calls. Become *the* expert so users trust you.
- Pick a narrow initial **customer segment** to optimize for.

## Who to interview matters as much as what you ask (Shear)

- Figure out the **real target user** (Twitch: broadcasters, not viewers — creators pull the audience). No recipe; use judgment.
- At the start, talk to the **broadest set** — not just obvious users but everyone critical to success: buyers/payers, admins, influencers (a student notes app also needs college IT, administrators, parents). Talk to the **extremes** (6 Stanford students ≠ 6 high-schoolers ≠ 6 parents). ~**6–8 per group** and you hit diminishing returns — so make groups diverse.
- **Three groups reveal different things:**
  - Your **existing users** → small feature tweaks (they tolerate the big problems, so those tweaks usually aren't the real issues).
  - **Competitors' users** → what's so bad it made them choose elsewhere (Twitch: money/revshare, video stability, global access).
  - **Non-users** → the most important; what *blocks market expansion* (can't broadcast, computer too slow, fear of leaking strategy). Twitch poured resources into things *no one literally asked for* because they addressed the underlying goals, then built broadcasting into Xbox/PS4.

## How to run the interview

- Ask about their **current behavior and problems, NOT features.** Users suffer the "faster horse / horseless carriage" effect. A real person requesting a feature feels compelling but is misleading. If you don't hear a **big blocker / real pain**, that's a negative sign.
- **Don't show your product. Don't pitch your pet feature** ("would you pay for a subscription?" → false yeses). Ask behavior questions instead (Rahman: "how much music do you listen to with others? how do you play it?"; iPod = "1,000 songs in your pocket," not "want a digital music player?").
- **Skype or in-person, not email** — you lose "interesting, tell me more" detective mode; people fill silence with gold. **Record** (with consent) — replaying interviews to your team beats "trust me, I talked to users" for buy-in.
- **Don't just talk to who's easy to reach** (your forum) — dig for the *right* people (they're usually flattered to be asked). **Don't compensate** — if they don't care enough to talk, wrong target.
- **Read between the lines:** users give feature requests; extract the underlying **goal/problem.**

## Validating an idea/feature before building

- You **can't** ask "is this feature good?" (they'll say yes and never switch). Instead: **just build a hacked MVP** and ship it, or **draw the workflow** and put it in front of people.
- Best validator = **sales/money** ("if you're not $5-excited, you're not excited"; get a credit card or prepay commitment).

## Feedback loop & honesty (Cheung)

- Track **retention** (cohort analysis); leading indicators = reviews/ratings/NPS. Surveys only catch lovers/haters — meet people in person (make it a conversation, not an inquisition; coffee/drinks).
- **Honesty curve:** strangers give the most honest feedback, especially when **paying**. Get to paid quickly for truthful signal.
- Build an engine: feedback → product decisions → back to users. **Tighten the loop** (10%/week compounds). Founders do sales & support themselves — put no one between you and users. **Close the loop**: go back to the exact people you interviewed once you've shipped the fix — they become your first converts.

## Keep doing it forever

The pool you care about **shifts over time** (Twitch later added game publishers). Starting, succeeding, then stopping makes your next features worse. Data/analytics tell you *where* users drop off, not *what* problem to solve — interviews are for the "what."

## Exit gate → Stage 4

Advance when: you can name the target user and their real problem in one sentence, you've interviewed the right (diverse) people including non-users, and you've heard a real, painful, recurring problem worth solving.
