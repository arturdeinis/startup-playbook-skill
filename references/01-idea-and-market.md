# Stage 1 — Idea & Market

*Sources: L1 (Altman), L3 (Graham), L5 (Thiel), L12 (Levie).*

The idea comes first. "Idea" is broad: the product **plus** market size + growth, growth strategy, defensibility/monopoly, and "why now." Plans are worthless but the *exercise* of planning is invaluable, and long-term thinking is rare enough to be an edge.

## How to get ideas (Graham)

Don't try to think of startup ideas directly — you'll generate plausible-but-bad ones. Instead, turn your mind into one that has ideas *unconsciously*:
1. Learn a lot about things that matter.
2. Work on problems that genuinely interest you.
3. With people you like and respect (← this is also how you get cofounders).

Get to the **leading edge of a technology** and "live in the future" — the things that ought to exist become obvious to you. The best ideas start as **side projects** that don't look like companies (Google, Facebook, Apple, Twitter). Build something **you yourself need** if you can — otherwise get uncomfortably close to customers (work in their office; talk multiple times a day).

## The idea checklist (score the idea against these)

- **Mission-oriented.** Would you give it 10 years? Mission-driven companies get more focus, attract talent and outside help, and it's easier to found a *hard* startup than an easy one (people rally to hard, important problems; derivative clones excite no one).
- **Sounds bad but is actually good.** The best ideas look terrible at first (13th search engine; social network only for college students; sleep on a stranger's couch) → less competition. You want to say: "I know it sounds like a bad idea, but here's specifically why it's great." Conviction + willingness to ignore naysayers (a fine line: right on one side, crazy on the other).
- **A small market you can monopolize, then expand** (see Thiel below).
- **Market growth rate > current size.** You can change everything except the market; you can't create one that doesn't want to exist. Prefer a small, fast-growing market (desperate customers tolerate an imperfect, improving product) over a big, slow one.
- **A crisp "Why now?"** (Sequoia) — why is this the perfect moment; why couldn't it be done 2 years ago; why is 2 years too late. Look for the enabling **technology shift** (Levie): the widest gap between how things are done and how they *could* be done now that some cost dropped / capability arrived. Revisit old ideas that failed for being too early.
- **Easy to explain in one sentence.** If it takes more, it's probably too complicated. Avoid clones with a made-up differentiator ("X, but beautifully designed").
- **Difficult to replicate someday** (defensibility).

## Monopoly theory (Thiel) — "Competition is for losers"

- Value = **create X dollars of value AND capture Y% of X.** X and Y are independent. Airlines create huge X but capture ~0; Google captures a lot. Aim to be in a business that *captures* value.
- Two kinds of business: **perfectly competitive** (make no money) vs. **monopoly** (durable, profitable). Almost nothing in between. People lie about which they're in — monopolists pretend they're in a huge competitive market; competitive firms pretend they're unique in a tiny one. Ask: **what is the *real* market?**
- **Start small, dominate, expand in concentric circles.** Amazon (books), eBay (Pez), PayPal (eBay power sellers, ~20k), Facebook (Harvard). Going after a giant market on day one usually means you mis-defined the category = too much competition (clean-tech died starting with trillion-dollar markets).
- **4 durable-monopoly characteristics:** (1) proprietary tech ~10x better on a key dimension (or brand-new); (2) network effects (hard to start — must be valuable to the first user); (3) economies of scale (software ≈ zero marginal cost); (4) brand.
- **Be the *last* mover, not the first.** Most of a tech company's value sits far in the future → durability > current growth rate. "Begin by studying the endgame": why will you still lead in 10–20 years?
- Competition is a psychological trap (humans imitate; we treat it as validation and fight over small stakes). Don't crowd through the tiny door everyone's rushing — go around to the open gate.

> Tension to hold: Thiel is skeptical of pure lean-startup iteration and favors a bold "quantum improvement" + conviction. The talk-to-users lectures favor tight iteration. Reconcile via Hoffman's investment thesis: hold a strong vision (true north) while letting data inside that framework refine the path.

## Vertical / enterprise idea patterns (Levie)

Every industry is getting a technology component. To attack one: spot the enabling shift → **start intentionally small** (a wedge that slips into gaps incumbents leave, then expand) → find **asymmetries** incumbents can't/won't copy (platform-agnostic where they must be integrated; novel monetization — Zenefits takes insurer commissions instead of charging the customer) → target bleeding-edge **outlier** customers as early adopters → **modularize, don't customize** (platform + APIs) → keep **consumer DNA** so it sells itself, but still hire domain salespeople.

## Common mistakes to catch

- Thinking the idea "doesn't matter" and being pivot-happy. Great execution toward a terrible idea goes nowhere. Successful pivots are almost always into something the *founders themselves wanted*.
- Making the first version sound *big*. It should take over a small specific market and expand.
- Obsessing over current market size; ignoring growth and "why now."
- Building a clone with a cosmetic differentiator.

## Exit gate → Stage 2 (or 3)

Advance when you have: a mission-driven idea you'd give 10 years; a defensible plan to win a small market and expand it; a market that will be big in 10 years; a crisp "why now"; and a one-sentence explanation. Then lock down cofounders (Stage 2) and get in front of users (Stage 3) — in practice these run together.
