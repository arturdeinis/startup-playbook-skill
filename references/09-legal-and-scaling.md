# Stage 9 — Legal, Finance & Scaling the Company

*Sources: L18 (Nathoo/Levy, legal & accounting), L20 (Altman, later-stage advice).*

This stage is mostly "put it on a list and handle it at the right time." The founder's job is now **building a great company that does repeatable innovation over a long time** — the single hardest thing in business (most companies do one founder-forced great thing and never innovate again; Apple is the rare exception).

## Legal & accounting basics (do early, keep simple) — L18

**Keep it simple, standard, and organized.** Don't get fancy (a botched CT-LLC→Delaware conversion cost one company $500k).

- **Formation:** a **Delaware C-corp** (settled law, investors comfortable) — not a Florida/other-state LLC. Steps: file with Delaware → adopt bylaws/board/officers → sign IP-assignment so the company owns your inventions/code. Use **Clerky** or a startup lawyer. **Keep signed documents safe and organized** — you'll need them under stress (Series A / acquisition diligence). Always separate "me as individual" from "me as the company."
- **Founder equity:** near-equal split; execution >> idea; **83(b) within 30 days, keep proof**; 4-yr vesting with 1-yr cliff (see `02-cofounders.md`).
- **Raising:** SAFEs/notes with a valuation cap (unpriced) vs. priced Series A/B; track dilution; raise only from **accredited/sophisticated** investors (avoid uncle/neighbor money that asks for it back); understand every term (see `07-fundraising-and-sales.md`).
- **Business expenses:** company's own bank account; only real business expenses (test: "would I be embarrassed if an investor saw this line?"). It's not your money — the founder who took investor money to Vegas is gone. Keep receipts.
- **Founder employment:** founders are employees and **must be paid** (≥ minimum wage) — working for free is illegal and creates liability; set up a **payroll service** (Gusto/etc.) and pay payroll taxes (missing them can mean jail). Unpaid wages become leverage in an ugly founder break-up (think of co-founder wages like a pre-nup).
- **Hiring:** classify employee vs. contractor correctly (1099 vs. W-2); **everyone signs IP assignment** (no IP = no company value); workers' comp + work authorization; use a payroll provider.
- **Firing:** fast; clear (no apologies), face-to-face + third party; pay all wages/vacation immediately; cut digital access; repurchase vested shares.
- Always know your **cash position, burn rate, and runway.**

## The management transition (~25 employees) — L20

- Flat/no-structure is optimal <20–25 people but **fails all at once** (fine at 20, disastrous at 30). Put in a **simple** structure: everyone has exactly ONE manager and knows who. Don't innovate on org structure (no matrix).
- Four manager-transition failures: (1) **afraid to hire senior people** (at scale, experienced execs are gold — everyone wishes they'd hired sooner); (2) **hero mode** (do all the work yourself instead of trading a few weeks of falling behind to hire); (3) **bad delegation** ("research it, I'll decide" vs. "you're smart, here's my input, *you* decide, I trust you"); (4) poor **personal organization**.
- **Write down the HOW (process) and the WHY (values)** on a wiki everyone reads → you "write the law." Highest-leverage thing founders skip.

## Good HR (speeds you up) — L20

Clear structure, career paths, frequent **performance feedback**, and **compensation bands** (someday everyone learns everyone's comp — bands keep it fair and cut negotiation). Keep giving lots of **equity** every year (refresher grants, stay ahead of vesting cliffs; plan ~3–5%/yr; get a real option-management system — Excel mistakes cost tens of millions). New rules at ~50 employees (harassment/diversity training). Monitor for **burnout** (marathon now). Hire a **full-time recruiter** once things work; build an onboarding ramp. Build **diversity** into the team before 12–24 months (a monoculture set early takes on a life of its own — diversity of background, not vision). Plan early employees' career paths proactively as the company outgrows their roles.

## Company productivity & alignment — L20

- Productivity drops with the **square of headcount** if unmanaged (communication overhead per pair). Antidote = **alignment**: a clear roadmap + goals repeated constantly. Test: pull 10 random employees — do they name the same top-3 goals? (Almost never — reiterate far more than feels necessary.)
- Stay **product-driven, not process-for-its-own-sake** (e.g. "ship something every day"). Transparency + rhythm: **weekly** management meeting, **monthly** all-hands (results + roadmap), **quarterly** plan, and **offsites** (underused, very valuable).

## Later-stage mechanics to remember — L20

- Get **books/accounting + annual audits** in place (~month 18, outsource); **collect all legal documents** while it's easy; set up **FF stock** at a later round (not seed — pushing for it early is a bad signal); file **provisional patents** ~11 months after launch (12-mo window, ~$1k, holds your place); file **trademarks** (US + major markets) and grab domains; hire **FP&A** (a great model reveals the business's knobs); hire a full-time internal **fundraiser** after the B round (better valuation, less dilution than a banker); set up **tax structuring** early.

## Founder psychology at scale — L20

It gets **worse, not better** — the swings widen. You go from underdog to a target of haters; make peace with it early. Commit **long-term (10 years)** deliberately. Take vacation. Fight losing **focus** (advising/conferences instead of building — the #1 post-YC failure). Don't entertain **acquisition talks** unless you'd sell at a low number (a major company-killer). The final cause of death for most startups is **founders giving up** — manage your psychology so you don't quit when you shouldn't.

## The long arc

Expect the **"trough of sorrow"**: press spike → decays to ~nothing → dips → wiggles of false hope → finally grows. Airbnb: ~1,000 days (3 years) before the graph turned up. Long, but doable. Don't hire a "professional CEO" — if you don't want to be long-term CEO, maybe don't start the company. If you must fail, **fail gracefully**: tell investors early, don't run out of money into debt, give employees severance and help finding jobs.
