# Stage 6 — Product/Market Fit & Growth

*Sources: L6 (Schultz), L4 (Cheung), L2 (Altman), L1 (Altman).*

**Retention is the single most important thing for growth** (Schultz). It comes from a great product + product/market fit. **Do NOT run growth tactics / virality / hire a growth hacker before you have PMF** — fix the product first. The #1 problem Schultz saw at Facebook and in startups: no PMF when they think they have it.

## Measure PMF with a cohort retention curve

- Plot **% of a signup cohort still active vs. days since signup.** If it **flattens (goes asymptotic to a line above the X-axis)** → you have PMF for some market. If it **decays to zero** → no business.
- You can estimate this with only ~**10k users**: look at the % of day-31, day-32, day-33... users who are monthly-active.
- **"Good" retention is reasoned by vertical vs. addressable market**, not guessed: e-commerce ~20–30% monthly is great; social needs ~80% early retention. Benchmark against comparable companies (users ÷ addressable population).

## The magic moment

The instant a user "gets it": Facebook = seeing a friend's face → "**get to 10 friends in 14 days**"; eBay/Airbnb = finding/getting that item or first payout. **Design onboarding to reach the magic moment as fast as possible** — that raises the retention asymptote (e.g. 60% → 70%).

## Operate for growth (Schultz)

- A startup **shouldn't have a separate growth team** — the whole company is the growth team and the **CEO is head of growth.**
- Pick ONE **north-star metric** aligned with the mission and hold everyone to it, publicly and internally: FB = monthly active users; WhatsApp = sends; Airbnb = nights booked; eBay = gross merchandise volume. Ignore vanity registrations. (eBay: paying affiliates for *activated* users, not signups, accelerated real growth — land users on the value, not the signup wall.)
- **Optimize for the marginal user** (the one not yet hooked / churning / resurrecting), NOT yourself or your power users. Build the product for power users; drive growth for the marginal user. Growth accounting = new + resurrected − churned.

## Growth channels (Cheung)

- **One channel at a time.** Execute hard for a week; if it works, ride it until it caps; if not, drop it (and revisit later as your economics change). Always iterate; be creative — find what no one else is doing.
- **Three types of growth:**
  - **Sticky** (retention): deliver a good/addictive experience. Measure **CLV** + cohort retention.
  - **Viral**: great experience + good referral mechanics. Sean Parker's model: **payload × conversion rate × frequency** (Hotmail = low payload, high frequency+conversion via the email footer; PayPal = low payload/frequency, sky-high conversion + $10 referral; Facebook grew by *word-of-mouth* virality, not email). **K-factor** = product of each step's conversion (import → invites sent → clicks → signups → imports); K>1 = viral, but pointless without retention. Remove friction at every step; repeating the same message lowers conversion (rotate creative).
  - **Paid**: spend to acquire only if **CLV > CAC** (CAC = CPC ÷ conversion). Segment it. Keep **payback time short** — ~3 months safe, 12 risky, >12 months = a leaky bucket heading for bankruptcy.

## Other tactics (Schultz)

- **Internationalize early** with a scalable/community translation system ("move slow to move fast"); prioritize *future* languages, not today's. Knock down barriers to growth.
- **SEO:** keyword research (optimize the word people actually search) → links from high-authority sites + good internal linking (Facebook added a directory → 100x SEO traffic) → table-stakes sitemaps/headers.
- **Email/SMS/push** hinge on **deliverability** first (be a good citizen; don't get spam-foldered), then open/click rate. Newsletters are dumb; send **notifications and triggered, in-context** messages — and only to low-engagement users so you don't spam power users.

## Set a growth plan and know when to pivot (Cheung / Altman / Hoffman)

- Set an optimistic-but-realistic **growth plan up front** (roughly double weekly early; manual user-getting sustains it). At the early stage a startup should always be growing.
- **Pivot criteria:** you can't grow, users don't stick, or unit economics don't work. If you work hard and see **3–4 weeks of no/negative growth**, something is fundamentally wrong. Homejoy was the 13th idea built out. Pivot *before* you crash into the wall — not after. (Hoffman: pivot when confidence in the thesis has fallen for a sustained period, after trying to rebuild it.)
- **Switching costs:** find the moment your product is clearly better; lead with 1–2 clear differentiators, not 50 small ones.

## Momentum is the lifeblood (Altman, L2)

Keep winning — a winning team keeps winning; a stalled one gets demoralized and quits. Regain lost momentum with **small wins**, not vision speeches ("sales fix everything"). When the team disagrees, ask users and do what they say. Establish an operating rhythm (ship regularly, weekly metrics review). Don't fear competitors until they beat you with a real shipped product (press releases < code < a great product).

## Exit gate → Stages 7–9 (parallel)

Advance when: a **flat cohort retention curve** (real PMF), a north-star metric everyone knows, and at least one channel that reliably brings retained users. Now fundraising (7), org-building (8), and scaling (9) run in parallel — but never stop building product and talking to users.
