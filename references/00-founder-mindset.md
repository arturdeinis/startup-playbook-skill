# Stage 0 — Founder Mindset & Readiness

*Sources: L1 (Altman/Moskovitz), L3 (Graham), L13 (Hoffman), L2 & L20 (Altman).*

Decide honestly whether to start at all, and go in with eyes open. Starting a startup is a ~10-year, all-consuming commitment. There are much easier ways to get rich.

## Should you start? — the one good reason

Only one reason reliably works: **you can't not do it.** That has three parts:
1. You're so compelled by a specific problem you'll work on it anyway.
2. The world needs it.
3. The world needs **you** specifically to do it (you're unusually suited).

The **passion/problem comes first, the startup second.** Wait until you have an idea you feel compelled to explore. If you're choosing between ideas, pick the one you think about most when you're *not* trying to think about work.

**Bad reasons** (all romanticized fallacies): it's glamorous; you'll be the boss; you'll have flexible hours; you'll make more money/impact than joining a rocketship. In reality you'll have the *most* bosses (employees, customers, investors, press), the *least* schedule control, and enormous stress. An early employee at a rocketship (Google Maps, the Like button) can have huge impact and upside without the pain.

## What it's actually like (set expectations)

- **All-consuming.** It takes over your life for years — likely a decade. Real opportunity cost. You can have a startup and *one* other big thing (e.g., a family), not many.
- **"It never gets any easier."** The problems change; the volume of worry doesn't (Graham). Even huge successes say this.
- **You're committed.** Leaving a startup you founded is a career black eye and harms your employees; you're effectively locked in ~5–10 years.
- **Managing your own psychology is the #1 CEO job** (Horowitz). The swings widen as you succeed — higher highs, lower lows (L20). You'll go from underdog to a target of haters; make peace with it early.
- **No balance while doing it.** Great founders put everything in (Hoffman: "if a founder talks about a balanced life, they're not committed to winning"). This can be for a bounded period, but during it you're all-in. Still: take vacation occasionally or you'll burn out (L20).

## Can you handle it? You can't tell in advance

A startup *changes you* — you're estimating not what you are but what you could become (Graham). Swagger vs. self-doubt doesn't predict who succeeds. If you're **terrified**, maybe don't. If you're merely **unsure**, the only way to find out is to try. Graham's advice to students: don't start in college — age ~20 isn't optimal; waiting *raises* your odds and preserves exploration/serendipity you can't get back. Best prep: "just learn" — get to the leading edge of something you're genuinely curious about.

## What makes a great founder (Hoffman)

- Not a superhuman good at everything — you need **a couple of superpowers** (a real edge), and cofounders who cover your weaknesses.
- Navigate apparent **paradoxes** with judgment — sometimes 100% one, then 100% the other: do-the-work vs. delegate; flexible vs. persistent; belief vs. fear; build vs. recruit; data vs. vision; long-term vision vs. near-term problem.
- Keep an **investment thesis**: write down why the (contrarian) idea is good and what you know that others don't. Constantly ask: is my confidence increasing or decreasing? Persist while it rises (even through valleys); pivot when it falls for a sustained period.
- Be **contrarian and right** — easy to be contrarian, hard to be right. Test: could a smart, informed expert disagree with you? If not, it isn't contrarian.
- **Infinite learning curve / adaptability**, no ego blocking it. Take intelligent, concentrated risks. Go where the network for your task is strongest.

## Founder psychology maintenance (do these)

- Build a **support network**; founder depression is real. The way through burnout is to address the failing things, not to vacation your way out (L2).
- Commit **long-term (10 years)** deliberately with your cofounders — few do, so those who do have a rare advantage (L20).
- Guard **focus** — losing it (advising, conferences, feeling great instead of building) is a burnout symptom and the #1 post-YC failure.
- Don't entertain **acquisition talks** unless you'd genuinely sell at a low number — they're a major company-killer (distraction, demoralization, cheap exits).

## Exit gate → Stage 1

Advance when: you have a problem you feel compelled to solve, you accept the ~10-year commitment and its costs, and you're prepared to manage your own psychology through it. If not, that's a legitimate answer — don't start yet.
