# Stage 7 — Fundraising & Sales

*Sources: L9 (Andreessen/Conway/Conrad), L19 (Bosmeny/Seibel/Caldwell/Younis), L12 (Levie).*

Two ways to bring in money: from **investors** (equity) and from **customers** (revenue — the best validation). Both reward the same thing: a business that's visibly working.

---

## Part A — Fundraising

### Mindset

- **"Be so good they can't ignore you"** (Steve Martin, via Andreessen). Build a business going up-and-to-the-right ("be the Twitter guys") and investors throw money at you. **Improving the business >> improving the pitch.** YC barely coaches pitching for this reason.
- **Bootstrap as long as you can.** Raising money is one of the *easiest* things you'll do (recruiting engineer #20, enterprise sales, and viral growth are all harder). It is **not** a milestone or success — just a step that lets you do the harder things. Fundraising ≠ success; smart people wrongly treat it as another application to ace.
- Only raise once the idea shows **signs of promise** (L20). Raising too early forces a panicked pivot to any plausible idea when the first doesn't work.

### What investors look for

- VC is 100% a game of **outliers** (~4,000 fundable/yr → ~200 top-VC funded → ~15 reach $100M revenue → those 15 make ~97% of returns). They invest in **strength, not lack of weakness** — checkbox deals rarely become outliers; extreme-strength companies often have serious flaws.
- Is the founder a leader, obsessed with the product (ideally solving a personal problem), a clear communicator, decisive? Can you describe it in **one compelling sentence** (25% of founders fail this)?

### The onion theory of risk (Andreessen)

A startup starts as a pile of risks (team, product, technical, launch, market, revenue, cost-of-sales, viral). Raising and spending money = **peeling away risk layers by hitting milestones.** Each round's pitch: "I raised X, hit these milestones, killed these risks; now raising Y for these next milestones." **Calibrate money raised/spent to the risk being removed** — don't just raise max and hope. The more capital-intensive, the more precise per round.

### Mechanics

- **Warm intros** (from entrepreneurs the reporter/investor trusts, or past investors) — the intro's quality predicts the outcome. Best path to Series A = through your seed investors (or an accelerator). Don't ask for an NDA (signals distrust). Get everything in **writing** (email a confirmation right after any commitment).
- Run meetings in **parallel, all in one week** — it's a sprint. Trick: "we're heads-down building for 2 weeks, can we meet in week 3?" → opens calendars AND signals you're not desperate. One founder fundraises full-time.
- Seed = price NOT set (**SAFE / convertible note** with a **valuation cap**; investor isn't yet a shareholder / no voting rights). Series A/B = price set. Raise only what you need; watch **dilution** (e.g. $2M on a $6M cap ≈ 25%, plus 20% for the A = 45% given away). Rough splits: seed ~10–15%, Series A ~20–30%. Don't over-dilute — a "destroyed" cap table (founders <~5%, outsiders too high) makes top VCs walk (demotivating for the team).
- Understand **every term** — the burden is on you: board seats (usually decline unless they truly add value), advisers (investors should advise for free; don't grant extra shares), pro-rata rights (their anti-dilution = your greater dilution), information rights (monthly updates fine; weekly/budget = overreach). See `09-legal-and-scaling.md`.
- Choosing investors is like a **marriage** (10–20 yrs). When things go well they don't matter; things always go badly at some point, and then who they are matters enormously. Good investors add rolodex + domain expertise. Test: do I respect them and learn from them in the first meeting? Do **diligence on investors** — you're selling part of your company.

### The pitch (Seibel) — keep it to 30 seconds and 2 minutes

Longer = more chances to say something they dislike.

- **30-sec (3 sentences):** (1) **what you do** — pass the "Mom test," simple language, assume they know nothing ("we let you rent the extra room in your house," not "a marketplace for space"); (2) **how big is the market** (real, looked-up numbers); (3) **traction** (or, prelaunch, evidence you move unusually fast).
- **2-min adds four:** (1) **unique insight / "aha"** in ~2 sentences — tell them something the big players don't know (watch for the real "huh"); (2) **business model** in ONE sentence (just say "advertising"/"direct sales" — don't hedge with a list); (3) **team** (call out only money-making accomplishments; else #founders, % technical, how long you've known each other, full-time, how you met); (4) the **ask** (know your instrument — note vs. SAFE, cap, amount, min check; use the right jargon here only).
- Raise from **strength**: flip it so investors chase you and you have a plan to launch/grow *without* their money — never "we can't do anything until you fund us."
- After the meeting: **follow up** (anything but a check/wire is a NO); create **"deal heat"** (demand for your round) to drive price/terms; **know when to stop** (fundraising is easier than building — don't hide in it).

## Part B — Sales (Bosmeny)

The "sales people" you'll hire early = **you, the founder.** "Talking to users" *is* selling. Founders' passion + industry knowledge trump sales experience. Early sales isn't Don Draper — it's a lot of phone calls; dedicate one founder to it full-time.

### The funnel: prospect → converse → close → revenue

- **Prospect** (who'll even take your call): only ~2.5% (innovators) will consider a no-revenue startup → a numbers game (Tyler reached 400 companies in 2 months). Channels: personal network, **conferences** (get the attendee list, email everyone in advance, book every minute), **cold email** (short: who I am, what I'm building, can we talk tomorrow?).
- **Converse:** the #1 rule — **SHUT UP.** Top salespeople talk ~30%, listen ~70%; ask questions ("why'd you take my call?", "how do you solve this today?", "what would your ideal solution look like?") to understand the problem better than they do. **Follow up** relentlessly (real deals take many ignored emails) — but push every prospect to a fast **YES or NO**; a pile of **maybes** kills you (a no is better than a maybe).
- **Close:** redline with standard docs; don't let pride drag you into 10 rounds over minor clauses — sign and move on. **Traps:** (1) "just build this one feature" ≈ a pass 9/10 times (there's always another) — instead put it in a signed agreement or wait for repeated demand; (2) the **free-trial trap** gives no revenue/validation/commitment — offer an annual deal with a 30–60 day opt-out instead.

### Match the sales motion to price

"Elephants" ($100k, high-touch — Salesforce/Workday), "rabbits" ($1–10k, must be **scalable**), "flies" (self-serve). Wanting to be a rabbit with an elephant sales cycle sinks you. Price to make the business viable. (Enterprise, per Levie: keep consumer DNA so it sells itself, but still hire domain-specific salespeople; land via the end user, then sell the org security/control/scale.)

## Business development / deals (Altman, L20) — 5 points

(1) build a great product first (nothing else matters); (2) develop a **personal connection** (don't be transactional); (3) create **competitive dynamics** (party A vs. B — the thing that actually moves deals/terms); (4) **persistence** (past your comfort point); (5) **ask** for what you want.

## Exit gate

You don't "exit" fundraising/sales — they recur. The signal you're doing it right: you can raise from strength when you choose to, and you have a **repeatable** early sales motion (or organic growth) rather than one-off heroics.
