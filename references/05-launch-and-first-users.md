# Stage 5 — Launch & Get Your First Users (Do Things That Don't Scale)

*Sources: L8 (Tang/Williams/Kan), L4 (Cheung), L7 (Hale), L10 (Chesky).*

There's **no silver bullet** for user acquisition. The companies with "dream growth curves" got their first users through brutal manual effort. Doing things that don't scale is one of your **biggest competitive advantages** — keep doing it as long as possible (it should be ripped from you, not given up).

## Test the hypothesis fast, then launch fast (Tang / DoorDash)

- Treat the idea as an **experiment.** DoorDash validated delivery demand with **PaloAltoDelivery.com** — a 1-hour ugly landing page with PDF menus + the founders' personal cell number. Real orders came in → demand validated before building anything.
- **Launch fast** (an hour, not six months). It's OK to hack things together (Square + a Google Doc + Find My Friends as the "dispatch system").
- Doing things that don't scale makes you the **expert in your business** (manual dispatch → the dispatch algorithm; emailing every new customer personally).

## Get the first users by hand (Williams / Teespring)

- It's the **founder's job** to do whatever it takes: 100 emails/day, cold calls, working your network, going to where users physically are. It's like pushing a boulder uphill — the first inches are hardest, then it rolls itself.
- **Don't optimize first users for time-ROI.** The first users take lots of hand-holding — that's essential, not wasted.
- **Don't give the product away free** — users must value it (free gives a false signal and doesn't convert the way you hope). (Consumer freemium is a separate model; this is about validation and commitment.)

## Channels to find them (Cheung / Bosmeny)

- You, cofounders, family, friends, coworkers → then online/local communities (Show HN, mailing lists) → **conferences** where your buyers gather (get the attendee list, email everyone in advance, book every minute).
- **Cold email** works if it's *short*: who I am, what I'm building, can we talk tomorrow?
- Do unscalable stunts to convert (Homejoy froze water bottles at a street fair; a startup pulled people out of the post-office line). Low conversion is fine to go 0→1→3.
- Only ~**2.5%** (innovators) will even consider a no-revenue startup → it's a **numbers game**. Reach a lot of people.

## Turn users into champions (Williams / Hale)

- A **champion** advocates for you. Create them by delighting with a memorable experience.
- **Talk to users constantly** — run customer service yourself (Teespring founders did it until $130–140k/mo; the Airbnb founders did nonstop phone support). Proactively reach out to **current AND churned** users (learn why they left). Monitor social/communities and **always make it right** — one detractor can undo ten champions.
- 100 people who **love** you > 1M who like you (Chesky/Graham). Reach them by doing things that don't scale — Airbnb founders slept at users' homes and hand-ran a free-photography service with a rented camera + spreadsheets before building any tooling; **perfect the experience for ONE person first**, then scale.

## Press (Kan) — a bootstrap, not a strategy

- Press is **not a meritocracy** and not a scalable acquisition channel — it's a vanity metric good for the first few hundred users. Don't obsess over it.
- First define **who** you want to reach and the **business goal** (national vs. local vs. industry trade). 7 story types: product launch, fundraising, milestones, business story, **stunts** (WePay's ice block), hiring, contributor articles. Ask honestly: would I read this if I weren't the founder? Be "**original enough**" (first in a category gets covered).
- Mechanics = a sales funnel: get a **warm intro** from an entrepreneur that reporter just covered; give ~1 week lead time; get **face-to-face or phone** (not just email — sunk-cost effect); pitch a memorized bulleted "ideal story"; follow up with collateral. **DIY over PR firms** early (firms give contacts/logistics but can't tell you what's interesting; $5–20k/mo). Keep reporter relationships fresh; pay it forward with leads.

## Speed over scalability (Williams)

- What you launch with won't be what scales — iterate fast; optimize for **speed over clean code** (Teespring duplicated the codebase to serve enterprise in days). Only worry about the **next order of magnitude** (at 10 users, plan for 100, not 1M). Necessity is the mother of invention.

## Exit gate → Stage 6

Advance when: you've hand-recruited real first users, some love you enough to tell others (champions), and you're ready to measure whether they *retain* — the true test of product/market fit.
