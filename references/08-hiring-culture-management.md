# Stage 8 — Hiring, Culture & Management

*Sources: L2 & L11 (hiring), L10 & L11 (culture), L14 (Rabois, operating), L15 (Horowitz, managing).*

Once you have PMF and are scaling, the founder's job shifts from *building a great product* to *building a great company*. This is the biggest transition in being a founder.

## Hiring (recap + scaling)

See `02-cofounders.md` for the first ~10. As you scale:
- Best source stays **personal referrals** (yours + employees'). Hire a **professional recruiter** early-ish (Pinterest's ~15th hire) once hiring is constant — but not so early you hire too fast (that implodes).
- Onboard fast: real work on **day one** (engineers commit day 1; new folks in real meetings day 1 — push off the cliff, don't ease in), plus fast feedback including on adapting to the culture. Learn people as people (aspirations, working style, how they like recognition). Announce every offer internally before making it (someone often knows something).
- **Retain:** keep people happy/valued (equity is central). Don't tell them they're failing daily. Praise the team, take blame yourself. Don't micromanage — give **autonomy, mastery, purpose** (Dan Pink). Do 1:1s. Assume you'll be a bad first-time manager and overcompensate.
- **Fire fast** (see `02-cofounders.md`).

## Culture (Chesky / Lin / Collisons / Silbermann)

- Culture = the **core values** and **actions** of each team member in pursuit of the **mission**. It's your first-principles engine for decisions (especially what NOT to do), alignment, and retaining the right people. "100 Best Companies to Work For" beat the market ~2x — culture pays.
- **Write down core values early** (Airbnb wrote them before hiring anyone). Start from the founder: what you value, who you love/hate working with (invert their traits). Values must support the mission, be credible, and be **uniquely yours** ("honesty/integrity" are table stakes, not differentiators). Zappos took a year to distill 37 → ~10; go deeper than one word.
- **Interview for culture/mission fit, not just skill** (use separate core-values interviewers outside the function to avoid bias). The smartest engineer who doesn't believe the mission won't pour their heart in. Make culture a **daily habit** (like fitness — you can't crash-diet it back).
- Founders = parents, company = child; build a team so talented it intimidates you. First engineer = a "DNA chip" — want diversity of **background** but **homogeneous values**. Hire people on a *calling*, not a job (the brick-layer building a cathedral).
- Culture's 3 hard truths: nobody talks about it, it's hard to measure, it doesn't pay off short-term. Hiring slowly/deliberately is the cost; ask "if I could hire anyone in the world, would I hire this person?" — every hire raises the bar.
- **Transparency** (Stripe): full information access forgives a lot as you scale — but build the tooling and social norms so it scales (weekly all-hands to communicate what email used to; norms for when to jump into public discussions).
- **Brand and culture are two sides of one coin** — what's inside comes out; sell your values, not bits-and-bytes ("Think Different"), or you become a utility sold at commodity prices. The CEO's core job = **articulate the vision** (→ set strategy → hire to the culture), repeated thousands of times.
- Scale via a **"startup of startups"**: autonomous, cross-functional teams that own their goal + metric + resources.

## Operating (Rabois) — you are an EDITOR

Manage the **output** of your org and adjacent orgs (Grove), not input (don't confuse motion with progress). Goal: build a machine "idiots could run, because eventually they will."

- **Simplify** — strike things with a red pen; distill to 1–3 repeatable things; don't accept "it's too complex." Each eliminated step ~+30–50% performance. Measure yourself by using *less* red ink over time.
- Ask **clarifying questions**; ensure a **consistent voice** across everything (website, PR, support) as if written by one person.
- **Delegate without abdicating** (both over-delegating and micromanaging are sins). Task-relevant maturity: more rope the more they've done the task → you have **no single management style** (good reference checks return a mosaic). Decision matrix (conviction × consequence): low/low → delegate fully and let them learn; high/high → don't let them make the mistake, but explain your reasoning (don't burn social capital with "you're the boss").
- **Barrels vs. ammunition:** most great people are ammunition; velocity comes from **barrels** (people who take an idea from conception → shipped, bringing others along — culturally specific and rare). Adding engineers doesn't linearly add output; adding barrels does. Find barrels by expanding someone's scope until it breaks (the intern who nailed the 9pm smoothies) and by watching who non-reports walk over to for help. Give them equity, promote, keep them. Rough ratio ~1 barrel : 10–20 ammunition.
- **Focus** people (Thiel at PayPal): ideally each person does exactly ONE thing. People default to solving B+ problems and procrastinate the hard A+ ones; one-thing focus forces someone to crack the A+ problem.
- **Scale via tools:** a founder-drafted **dashboard** (value prop + key metrics; success = % of employees using it daily). **Metrics:** measure outputs not inputs; use **pairing indicators** so teams don't over-optimize one thing (fraud rate + false-positive rate; hires + interviewer quality; output ÷ headcount to kill empire-building). Look for **anomalies** in the data (PayPal's eBay power sellers; LinkedIn profile-view vanity).
- **Details** ("The Score Takes Care of Itself" — the 49ers receptionist): sweat details users never see (Jobs' sealed Mac board) and the quiet ones (food, laptops, office — pick it yourself; it dictates culture). Take distractions off people's plates. Every good startup is a "cult" (get your own office). 1:1s every 1–2 weeks, agenda set by the *employee*. Do a **calendar audit** (list priorities, compare to actual calendar — recruiting is claimed #1 but rarely the biggest block). Promote domain-best individual performers into management (earns credibility). Effort/leading-by-example is non-negotiable.

## Managing decisions (Horowitz) — see it through the whole company's eyes

The one concept CEOs most botch: when making a critical decision, **sum up how every employee (including those not in the room) will interpret it** — not just your view or the person in front of you. Otherwise decisions have dangerous side effects.

- **Demote vs. fire:** a demotion feels win/win to the two people involved, but consider everyone — the demoted exec's VP-level equity now dwarfs engineers' (unfair signal); reports won't respect his authority. You're really signaling "what does it mean to fail on the job?"
- **Raises:** giving a raise to whoever asks tells the person who *didn't* ask "we don't evaluate performance; whoever asks, gets" → everyone now feels a duty to ask constantly. Fix: a **formal, periodic** process (quarterly/6-mo) that gathers input on everyone. Process protects the culture.
- **Firing:** be honest (usually it was your hiring/matching mistake). Take their job, not their dignity (your people are their references).
- When something important hits and you haven't thought it through from all perspectives, **pause** — "I'm taking this seriously; I need to think it through and come back." Don't bury it (the "Kimchi problem": the deeper you bury it, the hotter it gets).
- Toussaint Louverture as the model: he saw every decision from his soldiers', his enemies', the slave-owners', and the resulting *culture's* viewpoints — and built the only successful slave revolution in history.

## Exit gate → Stage 9

This stage recurs. Signs it's working: first hires grow into leaders, values are written and lived, decisions are made *through the culture* without you, and the company keeps momentum as it grows.
